# 📘 Chapter 3.1 — Word Embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText)

---

In earlier chapters, we represented text using:

* Bag of Words
* TF-IDF

👉 But these methods have a big problem:

* They **do not understand meaning**
* They treat words as **independent**

---

## 🔹 The Problem with Old Methods

### Example:

* “king” and “queen”
* “good” and “excellent”

👉 These words are **similar in meaning**
❌ But BoW treats them as completely different

---

## 🔹 What are Word Embeddings?

👉 Word Embeddings convert words into **dense numeric vectors**
👉 These vectors capture **meaning and relationships**

---

### 🔹 Simple Idea

Each word becomes a list of numbers:

* “king” → [0.2, 0.8, -0.1, …]
* “queen” → [0.25, 0.75, -0.05, …]

👉 Similar words → similar vectors

---

## 🔹 Why Word Embeddings are Powerful

They can capture relationships like:

* king – man + woman ≈ queen
* Paris – France + Italy ≈ Rome

👉 This is something older methods cannot do

---

# 🔸 Types of Word Embedding Models

We will study three important models:

1. Word2Vec
2. GloVe
3. FastText

---

# 🔹 1. Word2Vec

Developed by **Google**

---

## 🔸 Idea

👉 Words that appear in similar contexts have similar meanings

---

## 🔸 Example

Sentence:

> “The cat sits on the mat”

👉 Context of “cat”:

* sits, mat

👉 Model learns:

* Words appearing together are related

---

## 🔸 Two Approaches

### 1. CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words)

👉 Predict word from context

Example:

* “The ___ sits on mat” → cat

---

### 2. Skip-gram

👉 Predict context from word

Example:

* Input: “cat”
* Output: “sits”, “mat”

---

## 🔸 Advantages

* Captures semantic meaning
* Efficient

---

## 🔸 Limitation

* Cannot handle unknown words well

---

# 🔹 2. GloVe (Global Vectors)

Developed by **Stanford**

---

## 🔸 Idea

👉 Uses **global word co-occurrence statistics**

---

## 🔸 Example

* “ice” appears often with “cold”
* “fire” appears often with “hot”

👉 Model learns relationships using **overall corpus statistics**

---

## 🔸 Key Difference from Word2Vec

| Feature  | Word2Vec      | GloVe             |
| -------- | ------------- | ----------------- |
| Learning | Local context | Global statistics |
| Focus    | Prediction    | Count-based       |

---

## 🔸 Advantage

* Captures broader relationships

---

# 🔹 3. FastText

Developed by **Facebook**

---

## 🔸 Idea

👉 Break words into **subwords (character n-grams)**

---

## 🔸 Example

Word:

> “playing”

👉 Broken into:

* play, lay, ing, etc.

---

## 🔸 Why Useful?

👉 Handles:

* Unknown words
* Misspellings
* Morphological variations

---

## 🔸 Example

* “play”, “playing”, “played”
  👉 Similar vectors

---

## 🔸 Advantage

* Works well for:

  * Rare words
  * Indian languages
  * Noisy text

---

# 🔹 Comparison of Embedding Methods

| Feature       | Word2Vec | GloVe  | FastText        |
| ------------- | -------- | ------ | --------------- |
| Context       | Local    | Global | Local + Subword |
| Unknown Words | ❌ Poor   | ❌ Poor | ✅ Good          |
| Speed         | Fast     | Medium | Medium          |
| Morphology    | ❌ No     | ❌ No   | ✅ Yes           |

---

## 🔹 Real-Life Analogy

Think of learning word meaning like learning people:

* Word2Vec → based on nearby friends
* GloVe → based on entire social network
* FastText → looks at name + parts of name

---

## 🔹 Why Embeddings are Important

They are used in:

* Chatbots
* Machine Translation
* Sentiment Analysis
* Search Engines

👉 They are the **foundation of modern NLP**

---

## 🔹 Limitations

* Static embeddings (same vector always)
* Cannot fully capture context

👉 Example:

* “bank” (river vs money) → same vector ❌

---

## 🔹 Evolution

To solve limitations, newer models came:

* Contextual embeddings (BERT, GPT)

👉 These understand **context dynamically**

---

## 🔹 Summary

* Word embeddings represent words as **vectors**
* Capture **meaning and relationships**
* Main models:

  * Word2Vec → context-based
  * GloVe → global statistics
  * FastText → subword-based
* Much better than BoW and TF-IDF
